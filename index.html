Welcome. I am a PhD Candidate in New York University's Wilf Family Department of Politics and a research associate at NYU's Public Safety Lab. Before graduate school, I received a BA in Economics from NYU Abu Dhabi and conducted research in the Politics department.

The overarching goal of my current research is to describe the mechanisms by which electoral institutions shape criminal justice, including racial disparities. Specifically, my dissertation examines the influence of electoral incentives on the decisions of elected prosecutors and judges.

The first chapter develops an adverse selection model to formalize how racial, electoral, and policy-related considerations influence prosecutorial charging decisions. In the second chapter, I collect and clean criminal records from three large states (Texas, Virginia, and Washington), representing a universe of more than 38 million felony and misdemeanor charges from 2000-2020. I find that electoral concerns exacerbate the charging severity of Republican prosecutors and that Black defendants bear the brunt of this effect. The final chapter (co-authored with Sanford Gordon) examines changes in the behavior of California judges following the highly publicized recall campaign against Judge Aaron Persky. Using an originally collected dataset of over 20,000 sentences in six counties, we document a large, instantaneous increase in sentencing severity, which failed to mitigate preexisting racial disparities.
